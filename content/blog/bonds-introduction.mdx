Bonds spent the low-rate era of the 2010s as an afterthought for most retail investors — yields so low they barely covered inflation, with limited upside and the downside risk that rising rates would erode principal. The rate cycle that began in 2022 changed the picture materially. Understanding how bonds work is worth more now than it was five years ago.

## What a Bond Is

A bond is a loan. When you buy a bond, you are lending money to the issuer — a government, a municipality, or a company — for a fixed period at a fixed interest rate (the coupon). At the end of the period (maturity), the issuer repays the principal.

A Norwegian government bond (statsobligasjon) maturing in five years might pay 4% annually. Buy it at face value, hold to maturity, and you receive 4% per year plus your principal back. The only risk is that the Norwegian government fails to pay — considered negligible.

## The Inverse Relationship Between Price and Yield

The most important mechanic to understand about bonds is that their price moves inversely to interest rates. If rates rise after you buy a bond, the fixed coupon you hold becomes less attractive relative to new bonds paying higher rates, so the market price of your bond falls.

This is why 2022 was painful for bond holders: rates rose sharply and bond prices fell significantly. Investors who held to maturity were fine — they received their coupon and principal as promised. Investors who sold mid-term realised losses.

The flip side: when rates eventually fall, bond prices rise. Investors who bought in the high-rate environment of 2023–2024 locked in attractive yields and will see capital gains if rates decline.

## Credit Risk

Government bonds from stable countries are considered essentially risk-free. Corporate bonds carry credit risk — the possibility the issuer cannot repay. In exchange, they offer higher yields. The difference between a corporate bond yield and a comparable government bond yield is called the credit spread, and it reflects the market's assessment of default risk.

Investment-grade corporate bonds (rated BBB or above) carry modest credit risk. High-yield bonds (below BBB, colloquially called junk bonds) offer higher returns but with meaningfully higher risk. For most individual investors, staying at investment grade or above is appropriate.

## Why Bonds Belong in a Portfolio

The primary case for bonds is diversification. In most recessions, equity prices fall while government bond prices rise — investors flee to safety, driving up bond prices. A portfolio with some bond allocation tends to experience smaller drawdowns than a pure equity portfolio, which matters most for investors close to or in retirement, or those with lower risk tolerance.

The appropriate allocation depends on your time horizon and situation. A common rule of thumb — hold your age in bonds — is outdated for many modern investors. A 40-year-old with a 25-year investment horizon can typically tolerate more equity risk than that rule suggests. But some bond exposure from middle age onward provides valuable stabilisation.

## How to Access Bonds

Individual bonds require minimum purchase sizes that put them out of reach for most retail investors. The practical access point is bond funds — mutual funds or ETFs that hold diversified portfolios of bonds. A global government bond ETF or a short-duration investment-grade corporate bond ETF provides broad exposure with low cost and daily liquidity.

In Norway, bond funds held within a regular brokerage account are taxed on interest income annually. The ASK account structure does not apply to bond funds in the same way as to equity funds, so tax treatment is worth confirming with your broker.
